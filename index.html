I’m a Postdoctoral fellow at Brown University in the Brainerd Lab as well as a new mom! I’m a fish enthusiast, fluid dynamicist, and biomechanist. Specifically, I am interested in the relationship between functional morphology and fluid dynamics. In this, understanding the influence of specific movements on the fluid and the flow that is consequently produced that may indicate levels of performance.

My current research explores the intraoral flow during feeding, examining the potential alignment of food and efficiency of feeding in Notopteridae. Stick around to find out what I discover. You can contact me at corrine_avidan@brown.edu.

Power Amplification!

Animals are amazing and I am astounded by the biological mechanisms used for feeding. In my curiosity of the different mechanisms used, I have found an incredible system in an unexpected organism. Power amplification, or the ability to store potential energy in elastic elements to aid in faster and inherently more powerful movements, has been found in few organisms. These are typically found in insects, however I have discovered not only power amplification, but a dual system using two elastic elements in seahorse feeding! They used these combined elastic elements to simultaneously rapidly rotate head upwards while generating an extremely fast fluid suction, both are incredibly important for feeding.

Amazing Seahorses

Seahorses have this amazing feeding mechanism where they have modified the standard fish 4-bar system to contain 2 coordinated elastic elements. This allows for power amplification and the storage of elastic energy to generate movement speeds much faster than allowed by standard muscle contraction. In turn this allows them to create suction speeds 8x faster than fish of similar muscle size while simultaneously rapidly rotating their head upwards. Both of these factors together allow them to hunt one of the quickest prey, accelerating at speeds of up to 20 body lengths per second.
